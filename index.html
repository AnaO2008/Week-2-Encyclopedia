Asthma

Asthma is a medical condition that effects the lungs and can be life threatening.

How It Works

In the lungs, bronchioles are surrounded by a stron layer of smooth muscle. In asthmatics, this muscle is extremely sensitive, such that small stimuli, such as cold air, excersise, perfumes and other aerosols, or infection, can trigger their response. When the smooth muscle is triggered, it constricts the airway, decreasing the diameter and decreasing the amount of airflow. The lungs may also fill with a thick mucuss, further decreasing the space for air to enter and exit the lungs. Air becomes trapped inside the lungs, as fresh oxygen cannot escape the lungs or enter them.

What it Feels Like

Many asthmatics will describe the feeling of an asthma attack as an incredible weight upon their chest, liek attempting to breathe while an elephant is standing on them. They will wheeze and cough, often struggling to breathe. They may utilize accesory chest muscles, such as intercostal muscles, to increase their lung volume. In severe cases, an asthmatic may present in a tripod position, with panting and/or nasal flaring. They may experience lightheadedness or a feeling of impending doom.

Treatment

Many asthmatics will cary with them an inhaler, a small, handheld device that converts medicine into an inhalable aerosol. The medicine is most often albuterol, a bronchodilator which forces the smooth muscle of the lungs to relax, reopening the airway and allowing the flow of oxygen. After taking albuterol, some people may experience a loosening and lessening in mucuss, increased blood pressure, and shakiness.
